Reflections on Transitioning to an F1 Doctor

My name is Sam Redfern, and I am a Foundation Year 2 (F2) Doctor at Nottingham University Hospitals Trust. During my F1 training, I rotated through paediatrics, diabetes and endocrinology, and general surgery.

With the new cohort of Foundation Year 1 Doctors (F1’s) beginning this last August, I wanted to take a moment to reflect on one of the scarier moments of recent times – the transition from final year medical student to a foundation year 1 doctor.

The Transition from Medical Student to F1 Doctor

This is something that dawns on every final year medical student upon realising they have finally successfully navigated medical school. There is excitement and anxiety to this realization. After spending four, five, six, or even more years grinding through medical school— from early years embryology lectures to starting clinical placements and revising for your final OSCEs—you’ve now reached the beginnings of your career as a doctor.

I can still remember the fears I had before starting my F1, so through this blog post, I hope to discuss and pass on to you a few things I wished I knew before starting. I hope this can provide some reassurance to all new F1 doctors.

Remember, You’re Not Alone

If you’re going to remember anything when it comes to starting your F1 post, remember this—you are all in the same boat! It can be easy to fall into the trap of thinking that you, alone, are the only one struggling. Every F1 will find different elements of the job difficult, and every F1 will take a varying amount of time to settle into their new roles. Allow yourself time to build your confidence and adjust to full-time work—it won’t come instantly! Chatting with fellow F1’s can be helpful; either because they may share your struggle or they may know a way to help you.

Don’t Be Afraid to Ask

Everyone has to learn; you aren’t going to know how to do everything on day one or know where everything is on the ward. All the members of the wider MDT will likely have been there for a good amount of time, and asking them will be key. Everyone is happy to help you and answer any questions—they will know you are new and expect the new F1s to take a little time to find their feet and adjust to a new working environment.

I personally find a lot of the joy of medicine comes from the interactions I have with patients and my colleagues. Your colleagues are the ones who get you through a rough day, who come to support you when the day has been tough, and who pick you back up and get you back on the right path. Don’t underestimate how important they are to your day-to-day working life.

Along with this comes getting support from senior colleagues. If you are unsure of something clinical and are not confident in making a decision, please discuss it with a more experienced colleague. Although you have many years of medical school knowledge, some real-world situations may be new to you, and you may not have all the relevant experience to deal with a certain situation or make a fully informed and confident decision. This will come with time and experience, and you will never find out the correct way to handle a situation if you don’t ask!

Take Time to Step Away

Allow yourself time to take a step away from work and medicine. Some of you will start with very hectic and busy schedules, and if you are starting your first full-time job, this can take its toll. Late shifts, night shifts, and long weekends can be physically and mentally exhausting, especially when starting in a new position where everything may be unfamiliar and overwhelming.

It is vital that during your off time, you take time to sleep, relax, and do the things you enjoy. Don’t neglect the things that make you happy, whether that be going to the gym, playing sports, seeing friends, or any other hobbies you enjoy; these are essential for avoiding burnout at work. In addition, it is important to get your annual leave in relatively early; ensure you spread your leave throughout the 4-month rotation. Everyone will be different, but taking regular breaks from work by using annual leave will prevent everything from becoming too busy. Locum shifts can be available for F1 doctors if you have worked in the area previously; feel free to take them if you like, but again, ensure you give yourself enough time to properly recharge from your standard rota.

Start Filling Your Portfolio Early

Start filling up your portfolio early; take the opportunities when available to discuss topics, examine patients, or perform procedures in the presence of senior colleagues to start building up your portfolio. This will save you from bugging senior colleagues late in the rotation for your mandatory sign-offs. Doing a little bit of portfolio work once a week or so makes the whole year more manageable and prevents the stress of last-minute portfolio work as the ARCP deadline approaches. Use your allocated study days wisely for workshops, courses, taster sessions, or just to spend time building your portfolio.

Make the Most of Your Shadowing Week

Your shadowing week prior to starting your F1 post is invaluable. Shadowing an F1 for a week before starting gives you a week of supervised F1 work. Ask all those small questions then—no question is too silly. You will be shadowing F1’s who have been doing your specific job for almost 4 months and doing the F1 job for almost a year; they can give you the ins and outs of the job and teach you all the basics for the day-to-day working shift.

Get to grips with all relevant IT systems and ensure you feel confident with them prior to starting—this takes a lot of stress away from day one on the wards. Don’t shy away from any jobs that come up in this shadowing week—it will be you who must do them once the old F1’s have moved on, so get the practice in early. You can’t learn everything in a week, but do make the most of this shadowing period; you won’t get it again!

Key Takeaways

  • Be kind to yourself: Finding that work/life balance can be hard. Maintain hobbies outside of work and allow yourself to step away from the world of medicine.
  • You have support: Whether through your supervisor, other members of your ward team, your consultant, or your fellow F1’s, don’t be afraid to ask for help.
  • Make the most of your shadowing period: Ask those questions before day one!
  • Cut yourself some slack: You can’t expect to know everything on day one!
  • If you’re not sure, ask!
  • Do your portfolio little and often.
  • Be kind to ALL your colleagues: Not just other doctors.
  • You’ve got this!

As a closing note, I wish you all good luck for your first year of foundation training and all the very best for your future careers.
